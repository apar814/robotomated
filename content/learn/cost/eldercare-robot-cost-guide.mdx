Eldercare robots span a wide price range, from $250 tabletop companions to $25,000 facility-grade social robots. The cost differences reflect fundamentally different products serving different needs. A family purchasing a companion device for an aging parent living alone faces a completely different buying decision than a memory care facility evaluating therapeutic robots for a dementia unit.

This guide covers the full cost spectrum, including hardware, subscriptions, training, and the often-overlooked question of who pays: the family, the facility, insurance, or government programs.

## Companion Robot Costs

Companion robots provide conversation, reminders, cognitive stimulation, and social connection. They are the fastest-growing segment of eldercare robotics.

| Product | Purchase Price | Monthly Subscription | Annual Total Cost | Primary Setting |
|---------|--------------|---------------------|------------------|----------------|
| ElliQ (Intuition Robotics) | $250-$300 | $30-$40 | $610-$780 | Home |
| Amazon Astro | $1,000-$1,600 | $0-$25 | $1,000-$1,900 | Home |
| Miro-E (Consequential Robotics) | $2,800-$3,500 | $0 | $2,800-$3,500 | Home or facility |
| Cutii | $8,000-$12,000 | $200-$400 | $10,400-$16,800 | Facility |
| Pepper (SoftBank) | $20,000-$25,000 | $500-$1,000 | $26,000-$37,000 | Facility |

ElliQ has emerged as the cost-effective leader for home companion use. At $250-$300 plus $30-$40 per month, the total first-year cost of $610-$780 is accessible to most families. Intuition Robotics designed the pricing specifically to be comparable to a premium streaming service rather than a medical device, which has been critical to adoption.

The subscription model funds ongoing development, cloud services, and the conversational AI that powers ElliQ's interactions. Without the subscription, the device stops functioning beyond basic clock and photo display features. This recurring cost should be part of any long-term budget plan.

**Cutii and Pepper** target facilities rather than individual homes. Their higher price reflects larger physical platforms with navigation capabilities, group interaction features, and facility-grade durability. A memory care facility deploying Cutii across common areas budgets $10,000-$17,000 per unit per year, but each unit serves 15-30 residents in shared spaces.

## Therapeutic Robot Costs

Therapeutic robots are clinically validated devices used primarily in healthcare and eldercare facilities for specific therapeutic outcomes.

| Product | Purchase Price | Annual Maintenance | Replacement Cost | Clinical Evidence |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| PARO (therapeutic seal) | $5,500-$6,200 | $200-$500 | Battery: $400-$600 | 30+ peer-reviewed studies |
| Joy for All Companion Pets | $80-$130 | $0 | Batteries: $10-$20 | Limited clinical studies |
| Tombot Jennie (robotic puppy) | $450-$550 | $0 | Battery: $50-$100 | Early clinical studies |
| Miro-E | $2,800-$3,500 | $100-$300 | Components vary | Growing evidence base |

PARO is the gold standard for therapeutic robotics in dementia care. At $5,500-$6,200, it is expensive for a single-purpose device, but its clinical evidence base is unmatched. Over 30 peer-reviewed studies demonstrate reductions in agitation, anxiety, and depression in dementia patients. Facilities typically purchase 1-3 PARO units and rotate them through therapy sessions, serving 20-60 residents per unit.

**Cost per therapeutic contact with PARO:** A PARO unit used for 3 therapy sessions per day, 5 days per week, serving 4-6 residents per session, contacts approximately 60-90 unique residents per week. Over a 5-year useful life, the cost per therapeutic contact drops below $1.00. This is extraordinarily cost-effective compared to alternative therapeutic interventions.

Joy for All Companion Pets from Ageless Innovation sit at the opposite end of the price spectrum. At $80-$130, they are affordable enough for individual purchase but offer less sophisticated interaction than PARO. They respond to touch and sound with purring, meowing, or barking, providing comfort and sensory stimulation. The clinical evidence is thinner, but user satisfaction reports are consistently positive.

## Facility Deployment Costs

Facilities face costs beyond the robot hardware when integrating eldercare robots into care programs.

| Cost Component | Per-Unit Cost | Facility-Wide Cost | Notes |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------|-------|
| Robot hardware | $250-$25,000 | $5,000-$75,000 | 2-5 units typical |
| Staff training | $500-$2,000 | $2,000-$8,000 | 4-8 hours per department |
| Infection control supplies | $200-$500/year | $400-$2,500/year | Cleaning materials, UV sanitizers |
| Network infrastructure | $0-$2,000 | $0-$5,000 | Wi-Fi for connected robots |
| Program development | $1,000-$3,000 | $1,000-$3,000 | Therapy protocols, scheduling |
| Charging stations | $0-$500 | $0-$2,500 | For mobile robots |

**Staff training** is the cost most facilities underestimate. Robots do not integrate themselves into care routines. Activity directors, CNAs, and nursing staff need training on how to introduce robots to residents, facilitate therapeutic sessions, manage charging and storage, and clean devices between uses. Plan 4-8 hours of initial training per department, plus 2-hour refresher sessions quarterly.

**Infection control** is a non-negotiable ongoing cost. PARO units must be cleaned between residents using manufacturer-approved methods. Robots that circulate through common areas need daily cleaning protocols. Some facilities use UV-C sanitizing cabinets ($500-$1,500) for smaller robotic devices.

## Home Deployment Costs

Home deployment is simpler and less expensive, but families should budget beyond the device purchase.

| Cost Component | One-Time Cost | Monthly Cost | Annual Cost |
|---------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| ElliQ device | $250-$300 | $0 | $250-$300 |
| ElliQ subscription | $0 | $30-$40 | $360-$480 |
| Internet service (if not existing) | $0-$100 setup | $50-$80 | $600-$960 |
| Setup and support (family time) | 2-4 hours | 30 min-1 hour | Variable |
| **Total first-year cost** | | | **$610-$1,740** |

The most common hidden cost is internet service. Approximately 40% of adults over 75 do not have home broadband. Connected eldercare robots require reliable internet, so if the elder does not already have broadband, add $50-$80 per month to the cost.

Family setup time should not be dismissed. While ElliQ installation takes 15-20 minutes, the first week of helping the elder learn to interact with the device takes 30-60 minutes per day of coaching and encouragement. For adult children living remotely, this may require a dedicated visit.

## Insurance and Payment Coverage

Coverage for eldercare robots is evolving and varies significantly by product category and payer.

**Medicare:** Does not currently cover companion robots. PARO may be eligible for reimbursement as a therapeutic device when prescribed by a physician and used in a clinical setting, but coverage is not standardized and requires individual authorization.

**Medicaid waiver programs:** Several state Medicaid waiver programs for home and community-based services (HCBS) now cover assistive technology that supports aging in place. ElliQ has been deployed through Medicaid-funded programs in New York and Florida. Contact your state's Medicaid office to determine if eldercare robots qualify under your state's HCBS waiver.

**Long-term care insurance:** Generally does not cover robot purchases, though some policies cover assistive technology broadly defined. Review your policy language or contact your insurer directly.

**Area Agencies on Aging (AAA):** Multiple AAAs have purchased ElliQ devices in bulk for distribution to isolated seniors, funded through Older Americans Act appropriations and state aging budgets. If your family member receives services through an AAA, ask about technology programs.

**VA programs:** The Department of Veterans Affairs has piloted companion robots for veterans aging in place. VA-enrolled veterans should inquire through their care coordinator.

**Out-of-pocket tax considerations:** Eldercare robot expenses may qualify as medical expense deductions if prescribed by a physician for a diagnosed condition. Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.

## Cost-Effectiveness Analysis

The true measure of eldercare robot cost is not the price tag but the cost per outcome.

**Reducing social isolation:** Socially isolated seniors have 26% higher mortality risk and significantly higher rates of depression, cognitive decline, and emergency room utilization. If a $600-$800 annual investment in ElliQ reduces isolation, even modestly, the downstream healthcare cost avoidance is substantial. Studies suggest that isolated seniors cost Medicare $6,500-$12,000 more annually than socially connected peers.

**Reducing facility behavioral incidents:** Agitation in dementia patients drives staffing costs, medication use, and family complaints. PARO therapy reduces agitation episodes by 25-50% in clinical studies. For a memory care unit spending $50,000-$100,000 annually managing behavioral incidents, a $6,000 PARO investment with demonstrated efficacy pays for itself many times over.

**Delaying facility placement:** Every month a senior can safely remain at home rather than moving to assisted living saves $3,000-$8,000 in facility costs. If companion technology contributes to even a 3-6 month delay in facility placement, the cost savings dwarf the robot investment.

Eldercare robotics is not expensive when measured against the problems it addresses. The challenge is getting the right product to the right person with the right support, and ensuring someone pays for it. Start with the least expensive option that addresses the primary need, and scale up based on demonstrated results.
