**Quick Answer:** Heavy-payload AGVs (1,000 kg+) are the standard for automated material transport in automotive, steel, paper, and heavy manufacturing. They typically cost $80,000-$500,000 per unit and deliver ROI in 18-30 months by eliminating forklift damage, reducing labor costs, and enabling 24/7 material flow. Choose unit-load carriers for point-to-point moves, tugger trains for multi-stop routes, and custom platforms for oversized or ultra-heavy loads.

## What Qualifies as Heavy-Payload

In the AGV world, "heavy payload" generally means anything above 1,000 kg (2,200 lbs). This is the threshold where standard AMRs max out and specialized AGV engineering begins.

The heavy-payload segment breaks down into three tiers:

| Tier | Payload Range | Typical Applications |
|------|--------------|---------------------|
| **Medium-heavy** | 1,000 - 5,000 kg | Pallet transport, engine blocks, subassemblies |
| **Heavy** | 5,000 - 20,000 kg | Steel coils, paper rolls, large dies |
| **Ultra-heavy** | 20,000 - 60,000 kg | Ship sections, aerospace structures, castings |

## Heavy-Payload AGV Types

### Unit-Load Carriers

The workhorse of heavy-payload AGV fleets. These flat-deck or custom-top vehicles carry a single load from point A to point B. Most use lift-and-carry mechanisms — the AGV drives under a load stand, lifts the payload, transports it, and lowers it at the destination.

**Payload range:** 1,000-20,000 kg
**Speed:** 1.0-2.0 m/s loaded
**Cost:** $80,000-$300,000

**Common applications:**
- Automotive body-in-white transport between welding stations
- Engine and transmission line-side delivery
- Paper roll transport from production to warehouse
- Steel coil staging in service centers

### Tugger AGVs (Heavy-Duty)

Heavy-duty tuggers pull trains of carts, each loaded with components or subassemblies. A single tugger with 4-6 carts can move 2,000-8,000 kg of material per trip, making them extremely efficient for multi-stop delivery routes.

**Tow capacity:** 2,000-15,000 kg (across all carts)
**Speed:** 1.0-1.5 m/s
**Cost:** $60,000-$150,000 (tugger only; carts $2,000-$8,000 each)

**Common applications:**
- Lean manufacturing milk-run delivery
- Automotive assembly line-side supply
- Beverage and food distribution staging

### Custom Platform AGVs

For loads that don't fit standard configurations — oversized, oddly shaped, or extremely heavy — custom AGVs are engineered to spec. These include multi-wheel platform vehicles, caster-type AGVs with omnidirectional movement, and specialized carriers for specific product geometries.

**Payload range:** 5,000-60,000 kg
**Speed:** 0.3-1.0 m/s (varies with load)
**Cost:** $200,000-$500,000+

**Common applications:**
- Aerospace fuselage and wing section transport
- Shipyard block transport
- Heavy die and mold transfer in stamping plants

## Floor Requirements: The Hidden Cost

Heavy-payload AGVs demand high-quality floors. This is the most commonly underestimated cost in heavy-payload AGV projects.

**Floor specifications for heavy AGVs:**

| Payload Tier | Flatness (FF) | Levelness (FL) | Minimum Slab Thickness | Point Load Capacity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1,000-5,000 kg | FF35+ | FL25+ | 150mm | 25 kN |
| 5,000-20,000 kg | FF40+ | FL30+ | 200mm | 60 kN |
| 20,000 kg+ | FF50+ | FL35+ | 300mm+ | 100 kN+ |

If your existing floor doesn't meet these specifications, you have three options:
1. **Floor grinding and leveling** — $8-$15 per sq ft in AGV travel areas
2. **New slab pour** — $12-$25 per sq ft (only if existing slab is structurally inadequate)
3. **Route selection** — Map floor quality and design AGV routes along the best surfaces

In a recent automotive plant deployment, floor remediation cost $180,000 — 25% of the total AGV hardware cost. Always survey floors before finalizing the business case.

## Safety Systems for Heavy-Payload AGVs

When a 10,000 kg AGV meets a human, physics wins. Heavy-payload AGV safety is non-negotiable and governed by ANSI/ITSDF B56.5 in North America and ISO 3691-4 internationally.

**Required safety features:**
- **Safety-rated LiDAR** — Dual safety scanners (front and rear) with configurable warning and stop zones. Zone size increases with vehicle speed and payload mass.
- **Emergency stop buttons** — Accessible from all sides of the vehicle
- **Bumper sensors** — Physical contact detection as a last-resort stop mechanism
- **Visual warnings** — Blue spot lights, rotating beacons, LED strip indicators showing direction of travel
- **Audible warnings** — Speed-dependent sound levels, horn at intersections
- **Speed limiting** — Maximum speed automatically reduced based on payload weight and proximity to detected obstacles

**Stopping distances matter.** A 5,000 kg AGV traveling at 1.5 m/s requires approximately 0.8-1.2 meters to stop completely. This means safety scanner detection zones must extend well beyond that distance. Facility layout must account for these stopping distances at every intersection and crossing point.

## ROI Analysis: Heavy-Payload AGVs

The business case for heavy-payload AGVs typically rests on three pillars:

### 1. Labor Cost Replacement

A single heavy-payload AGV running two shifts replaces approximately 2.5 FTE forklift operators (accounting for breaks, changeovers, and absenteeism).

| Cost Element | Manual (Forklift) | AGV |
|---|---|---|
| Annual labor (2.5 FTE × $55,000) | $137,500 | — |
| Forklift lease (1 heavy-duty) | $18,000/yr | — |
| AGV annual cost (amortized over 7 years) | — | $45,000 |
| Maintenance | $8,000 | $12,000 |
| Energy | $4,500 | $2,800 |
| **Annual total** | **$168,000** | **$59,800** |
| **Annual savings** | | **$108,200** |

### 2. Damage Reduction

Forklift-related product and facility damage costs the average manufacturing plant $50,000-$150,000 per year. AGVs virtually eliminate this category:
- No racking impacts (AGVs follow precise paths)
- No product drops (controlled lift/lower mechanisms)
- No door/wall damage (defined clearance zones)

Typical damage reduction: 90-95%, saving $45,000-$140,000 annually.

### 3. Throughput Consistency

AGVs don't take breaks, slow down toward end of shift, or vary in performance by operator skill. A heavy-payload AGV delivers the same cycle time at 3 AM as it does at 10 AM.

Facilities report 10-20% throughput improvement not from faster speeds, but from eliminating variability, reducing wait times, and maintaining continuous flow.

**Typical payback period:** 18-30 months for a fleet of 3+ heavy-payload AGVs operating two or more shifts.

## Deployment Checklist

Before committing to a heavy-payload AGV project:

1. **Map your material flows** — Document every route, volume, payload weight, and frequency
2. **Survey your floors** — Hire a floor flatness survey (typically $3,000-$8,000 for a full facility)
3. **Assess your ceiling height** — Some navigation systems require clear line-of-sight to reflectors
4. **Identify integration points** — Where do AGVs interface with conveyors, lifts, production equipment?
5. **Plan for mixed traffic** — Define AGV lanes, crossing protocols, and right-of-way rules
6. **Evaluate power infrastructure** — Heavy AGVs require 48V or 80V charging stations on dedicated circuits
7. **Engage your safety team early** — Risk assessment per ISO 3691-4 should start during the design phase, not after equipment arrives

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the maximum payload for an AGV?

The heaviest commercial AGVs handle up to 60,000 kg (132,000 lbs), primarily in shipbuilding and heavy steel manufacturing. For most industrial applications, the 1,000-10,000 kg range covers 90% of use cases. Aerospace and automotive typically max out at 20,000 kg.

### How much does a heavy-payload AGV cost?

Costs range from $80,000 for a 1,000 kg unit-load carrier to $500,000+ for a custom 20,000 kg+ platform. Total deployment cost including floor prep, infrastructure, safety systems, and integration typically runs 2-3x the vehicle hardware cost.

### Can heavy-payload AGVs work alongside manual forklifts?

Yes, with proper planning. Best practices include designated AGV-priority lanes, defined crossing points with sensor-based right-of-way management, and clear visual/audible warnings. Many facilities transition to fully AGV-served zones over time, restricting forklift access to areas AGVs don't serve.
