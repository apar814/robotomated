You don't need a 500,000 square foot mega-warehouse to benefit from robotics. In fact, some of the highest ROI deployments we've seen are in facilities under 50,000 square feet — where a single AMR fleet can transform operations with a fraction of Amazon's budget.

Here's what actually works.

## Why Small Warehouses Are Different

Enterprise warehouse robotics is designed for scale: massive fleets, custom integrations, million-dollar implementations. That approach doesn't translate to smaller operations.

Small warehouses face unique challenges:

- **Tight aisles** — Many older facilities weren't designed for robots
- **Mixed workflows** — The same person might pick, pack, and ship
- **Limited IT resources** — No dedicated robotics team
- **Budget constraints** — Can't spend $500K to save $200K
- **Faster payback requirements** — Need ROI in months, not years

The good news? The robotics industry has finally started building for this market.

## The Three Robot Categories That Matter

### 1. Autonomous Mobile Robots (AMRs) for Picking

AMRs are the sweet spot for small warehouses. They navigate autonomously, bring goods to workers (or guide workers to goods), and require minimal infrastructure changes.

**Best option: [Locus Origin](/explore/warehouse/locus-origin)** — At $35,000 per unit (with a current price of $32,000), the Origin is the most accessible warehouse AMR. It carries 36kg payloads, runs for 12 hours per charge, and charges in just 30 minutes.

For a 30,000 sq ft warehouse processing 500 orders/day, a fleet of 3-5 Origin bots can:

- Increase picks per hour by 2-3x
- Reduce walking time by 50-60%
- Pay for themselves in 4-8 months

**When to scale up: [Locus Vector](/explore/warehouse/locus-vector)** — If you need to move heavier items (up to 270kg), the Vector handles pallets and bulk goods. Pairs well with an Origin fleet for mixed-weight workflows.

### 2. Truck Loading/Unloading

If inbound receiving is your bottleneck, consider [Boston Dynamics Stretch](/explore/warehouse/stretch). It handles 800 cases per hour during truck unloading — work that's physically demanding and expensive to staff.

At $150,000, Stretch is a bigger investment, but for operations receiving 5+ trucks per day, payback comes within 12-18 months.

### 3. Inspection and Monitoring

[Spot Enterprise](/explore/warehouse/spot-warehouse) can patrol your warehouse autonomously, performing thermal scanning, inventory spot-checks, and anomaly detection. At $74,500 it's not cheap, but for operations running 24/7 where walkthrough inspections are critical (cold chain, hazardous materials), it's surprisingly cost-effective.

## Implementation Roadmap

### Phase 1: Assessment (2-4 weeks)

Before buying anything:

1. **Map your workflows** — Track where people walk, what they carry, and how long each task takes
2. **Identify bottlenecks** — Usually it's picking, receiving, or inventory counts
3. **Measure your space** — Aisle widths (need 1.2m+ for most AMRs), ceiling heights, floor conditions
4. **Check your WiFi** — Most robots need reliable WiFi coverage throughout the facility

### Phase 2: Pilot (4-8 weeks)

Never go all-in on day one. Start with:

- 2-3 AMRs in your highest-volume zone
- A 30-day trial period (most vendors offer this)
- Clear success metrics: picks per hour, labor hours saved, error rates

### Phase 3: Scale (3-6 months)

Once the pilot proves ROI:

- Add more robots to cover remaining zones
- Integrate with your WMS for automated task assignment
- Train more operators
- Add complementary robots (e.g., Stretch for receiving + Origin for picking)

## Common Mistakes

- **Over-automating** — Start with one workflow, not five
- **Ignoring floor conditions** — Cracked concrete and debris kill robot wheels
- **Skipping WiFi upgrades** — Dead zones cause robot traffic jams
- **Not training the team** — Workers who feel threatened resist adoption
- **Comparing to Amazon** — Their robots cost $100M+. You don't need that.
