Space robotics is transitioning from government-funded research programs to a commercial market with identifiable revenue streams. The global space robotics market is valued at approximately $4.2 billion in 2026, projected to reach $8.5 billion by 2031, driven by the economics of satellite servicing, the return to the Moon, and the nascent in-orbit manufacturing sector. Unlike most robotics markets, space robotics is characterized by low unit volumes but extremely high per-unit value, with individual systems ranging from $10 million to $500 million.

This overview covers the three primary market segments, the companies leading each, and the technology and business model trends shaping the industry.

Satellite servicing is the segment with the clearest near-term commercial business model. In-orbit manufacturing, while smaller today, shows the highest growth rate as the economics of building structures in space rather than launching them become viable.

## Satellite Servicing: The Commercial Frontier

There are approximately 10,000 active satellites in orbit, with thousands more launching annually to support mega-constellations from SpaceX (Starlink), Amazon (Kuiper), and OneWeb. Satellites in geostationary orbit (GEO) represent $300 billion in on-orbit asset value, and many face retirement not because their electronics fail but because they run out of station-keeping fuel.

Robotic satellite servicing extends the life of these assets by refueling, repairing, or relocating them, at a fraction of the cost of launching a replacement.

### Northrop Grumman (SpaceLogistics)

Northrop Grumman's Mission Extension Vehicle (MEV) program demonstrated commercial satellite servicing in 2020 when MEV-1 docked with the Intelsat 901 satellite and took over station-keeping duties, extending the satellite's operational life by five or more years. The company's next-generation Mission Robotic Vehicle (MRV) adds a robotic arm and multiple mission extension pods that can service several satellites per mission.

The business model is straightforward: charging satellite operators $10-$15 million per year for life extension services, versus the $200-$400 million cost of building and launching a replacement GEO satellite.

### Astroscale

Astroscale is focused on the debris removal segment of satellite servicing. Their ELSA-d (End-of-Life Services by Astroscale - demonstration) mission validated magnetic capture technology for removing defunct satellites. The company's commercial ELSA-M service targets constellation operators who need to responsibly deorbit failed satellites to comply with emerging space sustainability regulations.

With the FCC mandating five-year post-mission deorbiting for U.S.-licensed satellites and the European Space Agency pursuing its own debris removal missions, Astroscale is positioned at the intersection of regulatory compliance and commercial demand.

## Key Companies in Space Robotics

### GITAI

GITAI is developing general-purpose robotic systems for space applications, including robotic arms for in-orbit assembly and maintenance, and autonomous rovers for lunar surface operations. The company's S2 robotic arm has been demonstrated on the International Space Station, performing cable routing and equipment installation tasks autonomously.

GITAI's approach mirrors the terrestrial humanoid robot thesis: build a general-purpose platform that can perform multiple tasks rather than designing a new robot for each mission. Their inchworm robot concept, which moves hand-over-hand along structures, is designed for assembling large space structures like solar power stations and communication arrays.

The company has raised over $160 million in funding and holds contracts with NASA, JAXA, and commercial space station developers.

### Astrobotic Technology

Astrobotic is a lunar logistics company building robotic landers and rovers for delivering payloads to the Moon's surface. Their Peregrine lander (first mission launched in early 2024) and the larger Griffin lander are designed to deliver commercial and government payloads to the lunar surface as part of NASA's Commercial Lunar Payload Services (CLPS) program.

Astrobotic's CubeRover is a compact, modular lunar rover platform that weighs 2 kg and can be customized for various surface missions including prospecting, inspection, and technology demonstration. The company is also developing the LunaGrid power infrastructure system, which will provide power to third-party assets on the lunar surface.

The company's revenue model combines NASA contract payments ($79.5 million for the Griffin VIPER mission) with commercial payload delivery services priced at approximately $1.2 million per kilogram to the lunar surface.

### Motiv Space Systems

Motiv designs and manufactures robotic arms and mechanisms for space applications. Their xLink robotic arm system powers the Sample Handling Assembly on NASA's Mars Perseverance rover, which has been collecting and caching Martian rock samples since 2021. The company also provides the robotic arm for Lunar Outpost's MAPP rover.

Motiv's competitive advantage is heritage: flight-proven mechanisms with documented performance in extreme environments. Their cold-operable actuators function at -170 degrees Celsius, enabling operation during lunar night conditions that would disable conventional systems.

### Maxar Technologies (now part of Advent International)

Maxar's robotics division built the Canadarm and Canadarm2 for the Space Shuttle and International Space Station, arguably the most famous space robots in history. The company's next-generation SPIDER (Space Infrastructure Dexterous Robot) system is designed for in-orbit assembly of large structures, including antennas, solar arrays, and space station modules.

Maxar's robotic arm technology is also central to NASA's On-orbit Servicing, Assembly, and Manufacturing 1 (OSAM-1) mission, which will demonstrate satellite refueling using a robotic arm equipped with specialized tools.

## Technology Trends

### Autonomous Operations

Communication delay makes teleoperation impractical for many space robotics tasks. Earth-to-Moon communication has a 1.3-second one-way delay. Earth-to-Mars delay ranges from 4 to 24 minutes. Robots operating at these distances must make decisions autonomously, including responding to unexpected conditions, planning grasps on irregular surfaces, and navigating uncharted terrain.

GITAI and NASA JPL are both advancing autonomous manipulation capabilities that allow robotic arms to identify, grasp, and assemble components without step-by-step human commands. This technology directly parallels the learned manipulation models being developed for terrestrial humanoid robots.

### Modular and Reconfigurable Systems

Space robotics is moving toward modular architectures where standardized joints, links, and end effectors can be combined for different missions. This approach reduces per-mission development costs and enables in-space reconfiguration. Motiv's xLink actuator system and GITAI's modular arm segments exemplify this trend.

### In-Space Manufacturing

Companies like Redwire (formerly Made In Space) are demonstrating that certain products are better manufactured in microgravity. ZBLAN optical fiber produced in space has dramatically fewer defects than terrestrial equivalents, potentially commanding prices of $1-$2 million per kilometer versus $100-$300 per kilometer for ground-manufactured fiber. Robotic systems are essential for operating these manufacturing platforms autonomously.

## Investment Landscape

Space robotics attracted approximately $1.2 billion in venture capital and government contract funding in 2025. GITAI ($160M+ raised), Astroscale ($400M+ raised), and Astrobotic ($340M+ in contract value) lead the funded company rankings. Government programs, particularly NASA's CLPS and OSAM initiatives and DARPA's Robotic Servicing of Geosynchronous Satellites (RSGS) program, provide the anchor demand that de-risks commercial investment.

The market's defining characteristic is long development cycles and high technical barriers to entry, but the potential market size, maintaining and expanding a multi-trillion-dollar space economy, justifies the investment for companies that can execute. The winners will be those that demonstrate reliable, autonomous operation in the unforgiving space environment.
