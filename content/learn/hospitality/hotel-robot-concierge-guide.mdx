Hotels face a structural labor challenge that is not going away. The American Hotel and Lodging Association reported 82% of hotels were experiencing staffing shortages as of late 2025, with housekeeping and front desk positions the hardest to fill. Meanwhile, guest expectations for service speed and availability continue to rise. Delivery robots and robotic concierge systems address both problems simultaneously: they handle routine delivery and information tasks around the clock without adding to the staffing burden.

Adoption is accelerating. An estimated 12,000 hospitality robots are now deployed globally across hotel chains including Hilton, Marriott, Aloft, and independent properties. This guide evaluates the leading platforms, the measurable impact on guest satisfaction and operations, and the practical considerations for hotel operators evaluating deployment.

## What Hotel Robots Actually Do

Hotel robots fall into two functional categories with distinct use cases.

**Delivery robots** transport items from a central point (typically the front desk, kitchen, or supply room) to guest rooms autonomously. Common deliveries include towels, toiletries, room service orders, welcome amenities, late-night snacks, and packages. The robot navigates hallways, calls and rides elevators, and contacts the guest by phone upon arrival. The guest opens the robot's compartment, retrieves their items, and the robot returns to base.

**Concierge robots** are stationed in lobbies or public areas to provide information, directions, and interactive guest engagement. They answer frequently asked questions (pool hours, restaurant reservations, local attractions), provide wayfinding, and in some implementations, check guests in or print room keys.

### Savioke Relay+

Savioke pioneered the hotel delivery robot category, and the Relay+ remains the most widely deployed hotel delivery robot in North America. The robot's enclosed compartment design provides privacy for deliveries (guests cannot see what other guests ordered), and the phone-call notification system ensures the guest knows when their delivery has arrived.

**Operational data.** Savioke reports that Relay+ units average 8-15 deliveries per shift in busy hotel environments. Average delivery time from dispatch to guest door is 3-5 minutes for a typical hotel layout. The robot achieves 99.5% delivery completion rate, with the remaining 0.5% requiring human assistance due to blocked hallways, elevator issues, or guest non-response.

**Guest response.** Hotels deploying Relay+ report that 70-80% of guests who interact with the robot rate the experience positively. The novelty factor drives social media engagement, with guests frequently posting videos and photos of robot deliveries. Several hotel chains report measurable increases in review scores and social media mentions following robot deployment.

### Bear Robotics Servi

Bear Robotics built its reputation in restaurant service and has expanded into hotel food and beverage delivery. The Servi platform carries up to 40 kg across three tray levels, making it suitable for room service delivery, banquet support, and restaurant bussing.

**Strengths.** The higher payload capacity enables delivery of full room service orders, multiple beverage orders, or housekeeping supply replenishment in a single trip. The open tray design is better suited for food presentation than Savioke's enclosed compartment, though it sacrifices delivery privacy.

**Hotel applications.** Bear Robotics units at Marriott and Hilton properties serve dual duty: running room service deliveries during peak hours and assisting restaurant operations during meal service. This dual-use model improves robot utilization rates from 40-50% (delivery only) to 70-80% (delivery plus restaurant).

Published data from hotel chains that have deployed delivery robots consistently shows positive guest experience outcomes.

The increase in repeat delivery requests is particularly significant. It suggests that robot delivery lowers the psychological barrier to requesting items. Guests who would hesitate to call the front desk for a second set of towels at midnight have no hesitation sending a request through an app that dispatches a robot. This increased engagement translates to higher guest satisfaction and more opportunities for upselling paid amenities.

## Financial Analysis

The economic case for hotel robots rests on labor reallocation rather than labor elimination. A 200-room hotel case study illustrates the model.

| Scenario | Without Robot | With Robot (1 unit) |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Night shift front desk/runner** | 1 FTE ($38,000/yr) | 0.5 FTE ($19,000/yr) |
| **Robot subscription** | $0 | $24,000/yr |
| **Amenity delivery labor (all shifts)** | Shared across 2 FTEs | Automated |
| **Staff reallocation value** | N/A | 1 FTE redirected to guest services |
| **Net annual cost** | $38,000 (night runner) | $43,000 (0.5 FTE + robot) |
| **Revenue from improved satisfaction** | Baseline | Est. $15,000-$30,000 (higher ratings, reviews) |

The direct labor savings are modest for a single robot, approximately breaking even when comparing a dedicated runner against a robot subscription. The value comes from three indirect benefits: 24/7 delivery availability without staffing 24/7 positions, reallocation of staff from delivery tasks to higher-value guest interactions, and the marketing and satisfaction boost from the technology experience.

Hotels operating multiple robots across larger properties see stronger economics. A 500-room resort with 3 delivery robots and 2 restaurant service robots can reallocate 2-3 FTEs while improving service consistency, generating net savings of $40,000-$80,000 annually after robot costs.

## Deployment Best Practices

### Infrastructure Requirements

**Wi-Fi coverage.** Robots require continuous Wi-Fi connectivity throughout their operating area, including hallways, elevators, and basement corridors that may have poor coverage. Conduct a Wi-Fi survey before deployment and budget $5,000-$15,000 for access point additions if needed.

**Elevator integration.** Cloud-based elevator integration (the robot calls the elevator via API rather than physically pressing buttons) is the standard approach. This requires the elevator vendor to install an IoT gateway, typically costing $3,000-$8,000 per elevator. Some older elevator systems may require controller upgrades costing $10,000-$20,000.

**Charging station placement.** Position charging stations in back-of-house areas near the primary dispatch point (front desk, kitchen). The station requires a standard power outlet and 1-2 square meters of floor space.

### Operational Integration

**PMS integration.** Connect the robot dispatch system to your property management system (Opera, OPERA Cloud, or equivalent) so that robots can deliver welcome amenities to newly checked-in rooms automatically and address guests by name on the touchscreen.

**Staff training.** Train front desk, housekeeping, and F&B staff on the dispatch interface (typically a tablet app), basic troubleshooting (restarting the robot, clearing navigation errors), and how to explain the robot to guests. Plan for 2-4 hours of training per department.

**Guest communication.** Inform guests about the robot delivery option during check-in and via in-room materials. Hotels that proactively promote robot delivery see 3-4x higher utilization than those that only offer it reactively.

### Managing Guest Expectations

Not every guest will be enthusiastic. Approximately 15-20% of guests prefer human interaction for all service requests. Ensure that human delivery remains available as an option, and train staff to accommodate preferences without making guests feel they are being forced into a technology experience.

The most successful hotel robot deployments position the robot as an additional service channel that enhances availability and speed, not as a replacement for the human hospitality that guests fundamentally value. When framed correctly, the robot becomes a memorable part of the stay rather than a cost-cutting measure that diminishes the experience.
